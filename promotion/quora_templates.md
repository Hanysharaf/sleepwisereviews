## Sample Answer: "What's the best mattress for fibromyalgia?"

```
**Most fibromyalgia mattress advice focuses on firmness — that's the wrong metric.**

What actually matters is pressure threshold at your 18 trigger-point sites. The clinical
threshold for tissue damage is 32 mmHg. Most firm mattresses exceed this at hip and
shoulder points in side-sleepers, which means sustained pressure all night on the exact
areas that are already sensitized.

**What to actually look for:**

**1. Pressure at hip and shoulder (not "firmness")**
Ask for pressure map data. You want below 30 mmHg at hip and shoulder in your dominant
sleep position. Memory foam in the 3-4 ILD range at the top layer achieves this.

**2. Temperature neutrality**
Fibro pain correlates with core body temperature. A mattress that traps heat raises your 
core temp, which directly worsens pain sensitivity. Look for copper-infused or gel-foam 
top layers, or latex (naturally temperature neutral).

**3. Motion isolation so micro-movements don't spike pain**
Fibro patients wake from pain caused by their own micro-movements. Memory foam beats
innerspring here — the foam absorbs movement instead of transferring it.

**What I'd recommend:**

For most fibro patients: medium-soft memory foam hybrid (memory top layer for pressure
relief, coil base for support without heat trapping). The Tempur-Adapt or Helix Midnight
Luxe hit this. Budget pick: Nectar Premier Copper.

I put together a detailed comparison of 7 specific picks here:
https://sleepwisereviews.com/posts/best-mattress-fibromyalgia.html

The pressure mapping section is the part most guides skip — it explains exactly why some
mattresses that feel comfortable in the showroom cause morning pain.
```

## Sample Answer: "Does mattress firmness affect sciatica?"

```
**Yes, but not in the direction most people assume.**

The instinct is "firm = support = less pain." For sciatica, that's wrong. A too-firm
mattress forces your lumbar spine into extension in side-sleep, compressing the L4-S1
nerve roots that are already irritated. You wake up worse.

**The mechanism:**

In side-sleep on a firm mattress, your hip doesn't sink enough — so your pelvis tilts
upward and your lumbar spine curves toward the mattress (lateral flexion). This narrows
the neural foramina on the down side, pinching the sciatic nerve.

The fix: a mattress with enough hip sinkage (typically 3-4 inches in the hip zone) to
keep your pelvis level and your lumbar spine neutral.

**What this means in practice:**

Medium to medium-soft hybrid or all-foam mattress. Specifically:
- Hip zone should be softer than lumbar zone (zoned support)
- Top layer ILD: 14-19 (soft-medium) for hip sinkage
- Coil height: 7+ inches for rebound support

I researched this in detail for a guide I wrote:
https://sleepwisereviews.com/posts/best-mattress-sciatica.html

The positioning protocol section explains exactly how to set up your sleeping position
alongside the right mattress to decompress L4-S1 overnight.
```
