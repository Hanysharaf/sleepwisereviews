Sleep Apnea Risk Factors You Have Not Considered (Beyond Weight and Snoring)

Weight and snoring dominate the public conversation about sleep apnea — and they are genuine risk factors. But they are far from the only ones. Jaw structure, nasal anatomy, alcohol consumption, hormonal status, and sleeping position all play significant roles in determining who develops obstructive sleep apnea and how severe it becomes. Many people with significant sleep apnea do not match the stereotypical profile at all.

    Beyond the Obvious: Why Sleep Apnea Gets Missed in Unexpected People

    Obstructive sleep apnea (OSA) affects an estimated 936 million adults globally, according to a study published in The Lancet Respiratory Medicine. The American Academy of Sleep Medicine (AASM) estimates that a significant proportion of these cases remain undiagnosed — and one major reason is that the condition is widely perceived as a disorder primarily affecting overweight middle-aged men.

    That profile describes one cluster of sleep apnea sufferers — not all of them. Women, thin people, young adults, and even children can have clinically significant sleep apnea. When the condition does not fit the stereotype, it often goes unrecognised for years while the consequences — cardiovascular stress, cognitive impairment, daytime fatigue, metabolic disruption — accumulate silently.

    Understanding the full range of risk factors is not just academically interesting — it determines whether someone gets assessed and treated, or continues to suffer from a diagnosable condition that was attributed to other causes.

    Jaw Structure and Airway Anatomy

    The geometry of your jaw and skull shape determines the space available in your upper airway. A retrognathic jaw (set further back than optimal), a high and narrow palate, or a short lower jaw can reduce pharyngeal airway cross-section independent of body weight. When the muscles that maintain airway tone relax during sleep — as they do in all people — a structurally narrower airway is more prone to collapse.

    Research published in CHEST journal confirmed that craniofacial anatomy is a significant independent predictor of sleep apnea severity. In studies of Asian populations, where craniofacial dimensions tend toward narrower pharyngeal airways relative to body size, sleep apnea was found at equivalent rates to Western populations at significantly lower body weights — demonstrating that anatomy, not weight alone, drives risk.

    Structural risk factor: A retrognathic (recessed) jaw, high narrow palate, or small chin are anatomical features that increase OSA risk independently of body weight. These traits are often hereditary.

    This has practical implications: if you have a family history of jaw or bite issues, or have been told by a dentist that you have an unusually high palate or recessed jaw, your sleep apnea risk is higher than your weight alone would suggest.

    Nasal Congestion and Deviated Septum

    Chronic nasal congestion — whether from allergies, a deviated septum, nasal polyps, or structural narrowing — increases sleep apnea severity by forcing mouth breathing during sleep. Mouth breathing during sleep bypasses the upper airway resistance and filtering function of the nasal passages, allowing the soft tissue of the throat to vibrate and collapse more easily.

    A meta-analysis published in Sleep and Breathing found that nasal obstruction was associated with significantly increased risk of both snoring and obstructive sleep apnea, even after controlling for obesity. Treating nasal congestion — whether with allergy medication, nasal strips, nasal rinsing, or in some cases surgical correction of a deviated septum — is a meaningful adjunct to sleep apnea management.

    People who only snore when they have a cold, or who notice their sleep apnea symptoms worsen during allergy season, are observing this mechanism directly. The connection between nasal airflow and sleep breathing quality is direct and well-established.

    Alcohol, Sedatives, and Muscle Tone

    Alcohol is one of the most potent acute triggers for sleep apnea episodes in people who are borderline or mildly affected. It works through muscle relaxation: alcohol suppresses upper airway muscle tone, causing the pharyngeal muscles to relax more than usual during sleep. This allows the airway to narrow or collapse at lower pressures than it would without alcohol present.

    Research published in the Journal of Sleep Research found that alcohol consumption in the 4 hours before bed increased the apnea-hypopnea index (AHI — the measure of sleep apnea severity) by approximately 25% in people with mild-moderate existing sleep apnea, and could trigger clinically significant events in people who were previously borderline.

    Benzodiazepines and other sedative medications have similar mechanisms — they relax upper airway musculature and suppress arousal responses, potentially allowing longer apnea events to occur before a protective arousal response is triggered. People using sleeping pills who have undiagnosed sleep apnea may be worsening their condition with the treatment they believe is helping.

    Practical implication: If you snore or feel unrefreshed after nights when you drink, the alcohol may be revealing mild underlying sleep apnea by removing the muscle tone that was keeping your airway open. This pattern is a reason to seek assessment, not just to reduce drinking.

    Hormonal Influences: Why Postmenopausal Women Are at Higher Risk

    Before menopause, women have significantly lower rates of sleep apnea than men — largely because progesterone and oestrogen appear to have protective effects on upper airway muscle tone and respiratory drive. After menopause, when these hormones decline substantially, women's sleep apnea rates rise sharply to approach male rates.

    Research from the Wisconsin Sleep Cohort, one of the largest longitudinal sleep studies conducted, found that postmenopausal women had a 3.5-fold greater odds of moderate-to-severe sleep apnea compared to premenopausal women of similar body weight. Women on hormone replacement therapy showed intermediate risk — lower than non-HRT postmenopausal women but still elevated compared to premenopausal baseline.

    Hypothyroidism is another hormonal condition associated with increased sleep apnea risk — it affects the musculature of the upper airway and reduces respiratory drive. The AASM recommends sleep apnea screening in patients with newly diagnosed hypothyroidism.

    Sleeping Position: The Back vs. Side Dynamic

    Sleeping position is one of the most immediately modifiable sleep apnea risk factors. Supine sleeping (on the back) allows gravity to pull the tongue and soft palate posteriorly, directly into the airway. Research consistently shows that sleep apnea events are 2 to 3 times more frequent in the supine position compared to lateral (side) sleeping in most OSA patients.

    This is well enough established that positional therapy — strategies to keep patients sleeping on their side — is a recognised first-line intervention for positional sleep apnea. Some patients with mild-to-moderate positional OSA achieve adequate control through positional therapy alone without requiring CPAP.

    Age and Muscle Tone Decline

    Upper airway muscle tone naturally decreases with age — an aspect of the broader loss of muscle mass (sarcopenia) that occurs from the fourth decade onward. As the muscles that maintain pharyngeal patency during sleep weaken, the airway becomes progressively more collapsible. This is one reason sleep apnea prevalence increases significantly with age, even in people whose weight has not changed.

    Research published in Sleep journal found that sleep apnea prevalence in adults over 65 was significantly higher than in younger age groups, even after controlling for weight and other known risk factors. The age-related change in upper airway muscle physiology contributes to this pattern independently.

    Thin People Can Have Sleep Apnea Too

    This bears emphasis because it is the most commonly overlooked risk group. Thin people with anatomical risk factors — retrognathic jaw, high narrow palate, large tonsils, deviated septum — can have severe sleep apnea despite having BMIs well within the normal range. Studies of military personnel and competitive athletes have documented significant sleep apnea in populations with low body fat and high fitness levels.

    If you are not overweight but experience persistent daytime fatigue, wake unrefreshed, or are told you snore significantly, do not discount sleep apnea on the basis of your weight. Request assessment. The STOP-BANG questionnaire is a validated screening tool your doctor can administer — it evaluates multiple risk factors beyond weight.

    Getting Tested: When to See a Doctor

    The AASM recommends evaluation for sleep apnea if you experience two or more of: significant snoring, observed breathing pauses during sleep, daytime sleepiness despite adequate time in bed, or waking with a dry mouth or headache. The presence of any single risk factor from this article — combined with unexplained fatigue — is sufficient reason to request an assessment.

    Home sleep apnea testing is now widely available and significantly more accessible than full in-lab polysomnography. Your primary care physician can order a home sleep test that measures breathing patterns, oxygen saturation, and respiratory effort during sleep. If the results indicate OSA, referral to a sleep specialist follows for treatment planning.
