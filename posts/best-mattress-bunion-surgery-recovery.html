Clinical Science: Hallux Valgus Anatomy, Osteotomy Types & What Your Mattress Must Do

Hallux valgus (bunion) is a progressive deformity of the first metatarsophalangeal (MTP) joint, in which the great toe deviates laterally while the first metatarsal drifts medially. Surgical correction — osteotomy — cuts and repositions the metatarsal bone to restore anatomical alignment.

Osteotomy types and their mattress implications:

  • Lapidus fusion (first TMT joint arthrodesis): Fuses the first tarsometatarsal joint. Most powerful correction for moderate-to-severe deformity. Requires 6–8 weeks strict non-weight-bearing (NWB) followed by 2–4 weeks heel-weight-bearing only. The mattress must support safe NWB transfers for nearly 2 months.
  • Austin/Chevron osteotomy (distal metatarsal): V-shaped cut at the metatarsal head. Preferred for mild-to-moderate deformity. Typically allows immediate heel-weight-bearing in a surgical boot. Full forefoot loading returns at 6–8 weeks. Forefoot contact with bedding must be avoided throughout.
  • Scarf osteotomy (diaphyseal): Z-shaped cut along the metatarsal shaft. Fixated with screws. Partial weight-bearing in surgical boot from 2–3 weeks. 10–12 week total recovery arc.
  • Akin osteotomy (proximal phalanx): Often combined with other osteotomies. Corrects residual hallux valgus interphalangeus. Weight-bearing protocol follows the primary procedure.

Surgical boot on the mattress surface: The rigid surgical boot is typically 4–5 inches thick at the heel, elevating the foot off the mattress surface naturally. However, on a soft mattress, the boot sinks, tilting the ankle medially and placing lateral stress on the osteotomy fixation screws. A medium-firm surface maintains the boot flat, protecting screw alignment and the healing osteotomy site.

Forefoot elevation for edema control: Post-operative edema in the forefoot is the primary source of pain and delayed wound healing in the first 4 weeks. Elevation above heart level (calf supported, not heel or forefoot) reduces hydrostatic pressure driving fluid into surgical tissues. The mattress must be firm enough to maintain a wedge pillow under the calf consistently through the night without allowing it to shift and lower the foot.

NWB transfer mechanics: Getting in and out of bed is the highest-risk moment during NWB recovery. A collapsing mattress edge forces the operative foot to bear unexpected load during the pivot-to-stand motion. Reinforced perimeter coil edges that hold firm under seated body weight are not optional — they are a clinical safety requirement for the first 6–8 weeks.

Forefoot pressure avoidance — toe contact with bedding: Rolling onto the operated foot during sleep is dangerous in the first 6 weeks. Memory foam surfaces that allow deep sinking can cause the forefoot to press against the mattress during side-sleeping position changes. A mattress with responsive pushback (hybrid or latex) is preferred over deep-conforming foam for post-operative mobility safety.

Recurrence prevention: Hallux valgus recurrence is strongly associated with improper first MTP joint mechanics during the remodeling phase (weeks 6–12). Sleeping on a mattress that creates lateral forefoot pressure — either through poor positioning or surface contour — risks exerting deforming forces on healing bone during the osteotomy consolidation phase.

Recovery Phase Mattress Guide: Weeks 1–12

Mattress requirements change as the osteotomy heals and weight-bearing protocol advances.

Phase 1 — Weeks 1–2
Strict NWB. Edema peak.
Priority: edge firmness, foot elevation, minimal movement. Back sleeping only. Surgical boot flat on mattress.
Phase 2 — Weeks 3–6
NWB continues. Incision healing.
Priority: consistent elevation maintenance, boot surface stability. Begin cautious side-sleeping on non-operative side only.
Phase 3 — Weeks 6–8
Heel-WB in boot (Lapidus) or progressive load (Chevron).
Priority: edge support for assisted stand-ups with partial load. Pressure relief on heel increases in importance.
Phase 4 — Weeks 8–12
Transitional shoe. Forefoot load returns.
Priority: soft forefoot pressure relief. No more boot — bare foot on mattress. Avoid lateral MTP joint pressure during sleep.

Important: Do Not Rely on Mattress Alone for Forefoot Elevation

A wedge pillow placed under the calf (not under the boot heel or forefoot) is required in addition to your mattress for the first 4–6 weeks. The mattress provides the stable base; the wedge provides the elevation angle. A soft mattress cannot maintain a wedge pillow in position through the night — the wedge shifts, the foot drops below heart level, and edema increases. Pair any mattress recommendation here with a firm wedge pillow rated for post-surgical use.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can I sleep on my back after bunion surgery?
Back sleeping is the recommended position for the first 6 weeks after hallux valgus osteotomy. It allows the surgical foot to be elevated above heart level using a wedge pillow under the calf, keeps the forefoot completely unloaded off the mattress surface, and eliminates the risk of rolling onto the operated toe. The surgical boot rests on the mattress without pressing the forefoot or incision site against any surface.
How do I get out of bed safely when I am non-weight-bearing after bunion surgery?
Safe NWB bed exit requires a firm mattress edge that does not collapse under your seated weight. Sit at the edge, swing both legs over, and press through your hands and heel of the unoperated foot to stand — keeping the surgical foot completely off the floor. A mattress with reinforced perimeter coils maintains the stable platform needed for this transfer. Soft foam-only edges that compress when you sit on them significantly increase fall risk during NWB exits.
How should the surgical boot be positioned on the mattress during sleep?
The surgical boot should rest flat on the mattress surface with the heel making contact but the forefoot elevated slightly by the boot’s rigid sole. Choose a mattress that is at least 10–12 inches tall so the boot does not cause the foot to hang at the mattress edge. Medium-firm mattresses prevent the boot from sinking deeply, which can tilt the ankle inward. Place a wedge pillow under the calf — not under the boot itself — to keep the forefoot above heart level without placing pressure on the incision.
How long do I need to avoid putting weight on my toe after bunion surgery?
Weight-bearing protocol depends on the osteotomy type. Lapidus fusion typically requires 6–8 weeks of strict NWB followed by 2–4 weeks of heel-weight-bearing only. Austin/Chevron osteotomy may allow heel-weight-bearing in a surgical boot from day one, with full forefoot loading returning at 6–8 weeks. Your surgeon’s specific protocol governs — these are general ranges, not medical advice. In both cases the mattress must facilitate safe NWB transfers for at least the first 6 weeks.
Does mattress firmness affect post-bunion surgery swelling?
Indirectly, yes. A mattress that is too soft causes the elevated foot to sink below heart level when the wedge pillow shifts during sleep, reducing the gravity-assisted drainage that controls post-operative edema. A medium-firm or firm mattress maintains the elevation plane more consistently overnight. Additionally, mattresses with significant heat retention (all-foam constructions without cooling layers) can increase local tissue temperature at the surgical site, which correlates with increased inflammatory swelling.
