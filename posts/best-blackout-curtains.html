Why Light in Your Bedroom Is a Bigger Problem Than You Think

Matthew Walker makes a stark case in Why We Sleep (2017): the human eye is extraordinarily sensitive to light at night. Even dim ambient light — the kind that creeps around the edges of inadequate curtains — measurably suppresses melatonin and pushes back your sleep onset time. In urban bedrooms, streetlights, car headlights, and illuminated signage can create a surprisingly bright sleep environment without you consciously noticing.

The fix sounds simple: proper blackout curtains. The challenge is that the curtain industry uses "blackout" as a marketing term with no standard definition. A curtain that blocks 85% of light is technically "blackout" by many manufacturers' standards — but 15% of streetlight leaking into your bedroom is still significant melatonin suppression.

🌑 True blackout test: Hold the fabric up to a bright light (a lamp or daylight). If you can see any glow through it, it will leak light into your bedroom. Proper blackout fabric should be completely opaque.

The Gap Problem: Why Even Good Curtains Fail

Even genuinely opaque fabric can fail if the curtain doesn't cover the full window opening. The most common failure modes are:

  • Side gaps: Curtains that don't extend past the window frame allow light to flood in around the edges
  • Top gaps: Curtain rods mounted too close to the window let light in from above
  • Bottom gaps: Curtains too short for the window let light seep in from floor level

The rule: mount your curtain rod at least 6 inches above the window frame and extend it at least 6 inches beyond the frame on each side. Buy curtains that reach the floor, not the window sill.

Installation Tips for Maximum Blackout

The best blackout curtains in the world underperform if installed incorrectly. These installation rules make a bigger difference than the choice of curtain brand:

  • Extend the rod 6–8 inches beyond each side of the window frame. This prevents side-gap light leakage — the most common failure mode.
  • Mount the rod 6 inches above the window frame, closer to the ceiling if possible. High mounting eliminates the top gap and makes windows appear larger.
  • Buy curtains that reach the floor, not the sill. Floor-length curtains block bottom-gap light that sill-length panels miss entirely.
  • Use a curtain with return panels or add a center overlap. Where two curtain panels meet in the middle is the most common light leak point. Panels that wrap around the rod ends to the wall eliminate side gaps completely.
💡 The morning test: After hanging your blackout curtains, close them in the morning and stand in the room for 30 seconds. Your eyes will dark-adapt and reveal any light leaks you wouldn't notice in the dark. Mark these gaps and address them before night one.
