The most common cause of neck stiffness after sleep isn't how you sleep — it's what you're sleeping on. A pillow that's too tall, too flat, or too firm keeps your cervical spine in a stressed position for 7-8 hours. Matching pillow loft and firmness to your sleep position is the first fix most people haven't tried.

Pillow Loft Guide: What Height You Actually Need

Pillow loft (height) must keep your cervical spine neutral — ears aligned with shoulders, no lateral flexion or extension.

  • Side sleepers: 4-6 inch loft. You need to fill the distance from your shoulder to your ear. If your shoulder is wider, you need a higher loft.
  • Back sleepers: 2-4 inch loft. Lower is usually better — you need support for the natural curve, not elevation. Cervical contour pillows work exceptionally well here.
  • Stomach sleepers: As thin as possible (or none). Any loft forces neck extension and rotation for hours.
  • Combination sleepers: Adjustable fill pillows — shredded foam or latex — let you customize thickness as you move.
Quick test: Stand against a wall. The gap between the wall and the back of your head is approximately your ideal pillow loft for back sleeping. For side sleeping, add the width of your shoulder minus half your head width.

What Doesn't Help (Common Mistakes)

  • Stacking pillows: Creates unpredictable loft and often results in neck flexion
  • Fluffy down or polyester pillows: Compress under head weight, providing no structural support after the first hour
  • Orthopaedic pillows marketed for "all positions": No single contour works for side and back sleeping simultaneously
  • Expensive doesn't mean correct: A $300 pillow at the wrong loft will cause more neck pain than a $40 pillow at the right loft
