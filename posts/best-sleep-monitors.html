Best Bedside Sleep Monitors
(Beyond Wrist Trackers)

📅 February 2021  ·  ⏱ 7 min read  ·  🔄 Updated Mar 2026

Wrist trackers miss up to 30% of nighttime awakenings. Bedside monitors use radar, sonar, and ballistocardiography to track your sleep without touching you — and the data is fundamentally different.

Every major fitness brand has a wrist tracker that claims to map your sleep. But if you've ever woken feeling exhausted despite your app reporting "8h 20m — excellent recovery," you've encountered the core limitation of wrist-worn actigraphy: it measures movement. What it can't reliably detect are the quiet, still moments when your body is in fact disturbed — micro-arousals, breathing irregularities, cardiac-rhythm fluctuations — because you haven't physically moved your arm.

The alternative is a class of devices that sit on your bedside table, slide under your mattress, or mount on a wall. They don't touch you at all. And the science behind them is, in several ways, more capable of capturing what actually happens during the night.

From the science: In The Power of When, sleep clinician Michael Breus, PhD notes that the most accurate consumer sleep data comes not from wrist-worn actigraphy but from under-mattress and bedside sensors that capture subtle body movements and micro-vibrations associated with breathing and cardiac activity during each sleep stage (Breus, 2016).

    Why Non-Wearable Monitoring Matters

    The appeal is practical as much as technical. A significant portion of the population simply won't wear a device to bed — wristbands feel constricting, rings are unfamiliar, and there's the nightly friction of remembering to put it on and keep it charged. Bedside devices remove all of that. You set them up once and they run every night, automatically, without any action on your part.

    But the more important difference is physiological. Bedside sensors are designed to capture signals that wrist actigraphy is structurally poor at detecting:

    • Respiratory rate: The rise and fall of your chest produces mechanical vibration in the mattress or detectable micro-Doppler shifts in the air around you. A wristband cannot see this.
    • Heart rate variability during transitions: The subtle cardiac-cycle signals that distinguish light NREM from deep slow-wave sleep are captured at the source — your torso — not your wrist.
    • Snoring and breathing pauses: Acoustic and vibration detection of snoring is a category that wrist trackers cannot meaningfully enter.
    • Micro-arousals: The brief (3–15 second) awakenings that fragment sleep quality without you consciously "waking up" leave a physical signature in your breathing pattern and body position, visible to bedside sensors.

    For people with suspected sleep apnea who want a consumer-level screening tool before a clinical sleep study, bedside sensors are the only consumer category even attempting to address that need.

    Key Takeaway

    If your primary concern is detecting breathing irregularities or potential sleep apnea indicators, under-mattress ballistocardiography (Withings Sleep Analyzer) is the only consumer-accessible technology that directly captures the respiratory and cardiac micro-vibration data needed. No wrist tracker can substitute for this.

    Accuracy vs. Polysomnography

    The honest benchmark for any sleep device is polysomnography (PSG) — the gold standard clinical sleep study that uses EEG, EOG, EMG, airflow sensors, and pulse oximetry simultaneously. Consumer devices are not PSG and should never be treated as clinical diagnostics. But within that caveat, the comparison is instructive.

    For sleep stage classification, PSG-validated studies on under-mattress sensors like Withings and SleepScore Max typically show epoch-by-epoch agreement in the range of 70–78% — comparable to or slightly better than wrist actigraphy, which averages 65–72% in similar studies. The difference is modest for stages. The difference is larger for breathing:

    • Under-mattress ballistocardiography can detect breathing irregularities with sufficient sensitivity to flag apnea-hypopnea index (AHI) patterns correlated with clinical thresholds.
    • Wrist trackers, with no respiratory sensor, cannot make this detection at all.
    • Radar and sonar devices detect respiratory rate reliably but are not validated for apnea event detection in clinical terms.

    The practical upshot: if your goal is better sleep stage data, any bedside device is comparable to a wrist tracker with lower wearing burden. If your goal is respiratory monitoring — either for apnea screening or general interest in breathing quality — only under-mattress sensors are doing meaningful work in this space.

    Who Should Use a Bedside Monitor

    People who dislike wearing devices to bed

    The most obvious use case. If you've tried a smartwatch or fitness band and found the sensation disruptive — or simply can't maintain the habit of wearing it every night — a passive bedside sensor removes all friction entirely. You set it up once. After that, tracking happens automatically with zero nightly input.

    People with suspected sleep apnea seeking pre-clinical screening

    Clinical sleep studies involve scheduling, insurance, and overnight lab stays. For the large population of people who snore heavily, wake feeling unrefreshed, or have been told by a partner about breathing pauses, a device like the Withings Sleep Analyzer offers a meaningful first data point. It won't replace a sleep physician's diagnosis, but it can document months of nightly breathing data and help you have a more informed conversation with your doctor about whether a formal study is warranted.

    Couples sharing a bed

    Under-mattress sensors designed for two people (Withings has dual-zone options) can track both occupants independently without either person wearing anything. This is genuinely impractical for wrist-worn solutions and not possible at all for single-unit wearables.

    Elderly users or individuals with medical conditions

    For users who may not reliably remember to wear a tracker, find wearables uncomfortable due to skin sensitivity, or have medical devices that interfere with wrist-worn hardware, bedside monitoring offers continuous overnight tracking without any compliance requirement.

    Bottom Line

    For most people who dislike wearables, the Withings Sleep Analyzer is the strongest recommendation: it is the only consumer device that combines under-mattress accuracy, breathing disturbance detection, snore analysis, and a well-designed app at a one-time price. For those already invested in the Google ecosystem, the Nest Hub's built-in sonar tracking is a free and surprisingly capable baseline that requires no additional hardware.
