The Science: How Melatonin Actually Works

Melatonin (N-acetyl-5-methoxytryptamine) is produced by the pineal gland in response to darkness. It is a circadian timing signal, not a sedative. Melatonin communicates to the brain that it is nighttime — triggering downstream processes that lower core body temperature, reduce cortisol, and create sleep-permissive conditions. This is why melatonin works best for circadian disruptions (jet lag, shift work, delayed sleep phase disorder) and is less effective for pure insomnia driven by anxiety or other causes. Meta-analyses consistently find that 0.5-3mg taken 30-60 minutes before target sleep time reduces sleep onset latency by 7-12 minutes and improves sleep quality — modest but meaningful effects. Higher doses do not produce proportionally better outcomes and increase the risk of morning grogginess (hangover effect) and circadian phase-shifting in the wrong direction.

Melatonin Dosage and Usage Guide

Start Low: The 0.5mg Rule

If you have never taken melatonin, start with 0.5mg. Most people who try melatonin and conclude "it doesn't work" have taken 5-10mg and experienced either no effect (because they are melatonin-resistant at high doses) or grogginess (because they have taken too much). The 0.5-1mg range is where research consistently finds benefit. If 0.5mg is ineffective after 1-2 weeks, step up to 1mg, then 3mg. Most users will not need more than 3mg.

Timing: The 30-60 Minute Window

Melatonin is most effective when taken 30-60 minutes before your intended sleep time. Taking it too early can shift your circadian rhythm in unintended directions. Taking it too late means it has not reached peak blood concentration at sleep onset. Fast-dissolve formats peak 15-20 minutes earlier than swallowed capsules, allowing a narrower 20-30 minute window. Set a consistent timer rather than taking it "when you feel sleepy."

Immediate-Release vs. Time-Release

Immediate-release melatonin addresses sleep onset difficulty — trouble falling asleep at the beginning of the night. Time-release melatonin addresses sleep maintenance difficulty — waking in the middle of the night and struggling to return to sleep. If your primary problem is middle-of-night waking, time-release is the appropriate format. If your primary problem is initial sleep onset, immediate-release is more targeted.

Third-Party Testing: Why It Matters

A 2017 study in the Journal of Sleep Research tested 31 melatonin supplements and found that actual melatonin content varied from 83% below to 478% above the labeled dose. Without third-party testing, you cannot trust the label. For sleep supplements, prioritize brands with USP Verified, NSF Certified for Sport, or Informed Sport certification. Thorne, Pure Encapsulations, and NOW Supplements all have verifiable testing programs.

Who Should Not Take Melatonin

Melatonin interacts with anticoagulants (warfarin), immunosuppressants, diabetes medications, and blood pressure medications. It is not recommended during pregnancy without medical supervision. People with autoimmune conditions (lupus, rheumatoid arthritis) should use cautiously — melatonin modulates immune function. If you take any prescription medication, verify with your pharmacist before starting melatonin supplementation.

Melatonin for Jet Lag: The Protocol

For eastward travel (advancing sleep time): take 0.5-5mg at the destination's normal bedtime for 3-5 days. For westward travel (delaying sleep time): take 0.5mg at the destination's bedtime for 2-3 nights. Bright light exposure in the morning at your destination reinforces the circadian shift — melatonin works best combined with appropriate light exposure, not as a standalone intervention.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the correct melatonin dose for sleep?

Research consistently shows that 0.5mg to 3mg is the effective range for most adults. Standard OTC doses of 5-10mg are 3-10x higher than what studies show to be effective. Higher doses do not produce proportionally better sleep and may cause daytime grogginess. Start with 0.5mg to 1mg 30-60 minutes before bed. Increase only if ineffective.

Can you take melatonin every night?

Short-term nightly use (2-4 weeks) is well-supported by research. Long-term nightly use has limited safety data, and some research suggests it may reduce the body's natural melatonin production over time with sustained high doses. Low-dose melatonin (0.5mg) is considered lower-risk for extended use. Use melatonin to reset a disrupted sleep schedule, not as a permanent nightly sedative.

When should you take melatonin before bed?

30 to 60 minutes before your intended sleep time is the evidence-based recommendation. Melatonin is a circadian signal, not a sedative — it works by advancing or reinforcing your sleep timing, not by knocking you out. Taking it too early (3+ hours before bed) can cause daytime grogginess the next morning. Fast-dissolve formulations peak faster and can be taken 20-30 minutes before bed.

Does melatonin work for jet lag?

Yes — jet lag is one of the most strongly evidence-supported uses for melatonin. 0.5-5mg taken at the destination bedtime (not your origin time) for 3-5 days after eastward travel significantly reduces jet lag symptoms. The Cochrane Collaboration's review of 10 trials found melatonin effective for jet lag prevention, particularly for eastward travel.

Is melatonin safe for children?

The American Academy of Sleep Medicine considers melatonin relatively safe for short-term use in children, particularly for sleep-onset difficulties. Doses of 0.5-3mg are typically used for pediatric sleep issues. Long-term use in children requires medical supervision due to limited safety data on effects on hormonal development. Always consult a pediatrician before giving melatonin to a child.
