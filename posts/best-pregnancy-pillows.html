Best Pregnancy Pillows for Every Sleep Position

📅 December 2020 ⏱ 7 min read 🔄 Updated Mar 2026

By the second trimester, every sleep position except side-lying cuts blood flow to the fetus. A pregnancy pillow isn't a luxury — it's the most practical tool for maintaining the SOS position through a full night of sleep.

Most pregnant women discover this problem around week 18 to 20: their body has a lifelong pattern of sleeping in one position, and now every instinct that once made sleep comfortable is working against them. Rolling onto your back compresses the inferior vena cava — the large vein running along the right side of your spine — reducing the return of blood to your heart and, consequently, blood and oxygen delivery to the placenta. Sleeping on your right side isn't dangerous in the early months, but as the uterus grows, left-side positioning becomes the clearer choice.

The result is a nightly battle against your own muscle memory. That's precisely the problem a well-chosen pregnancy pillow solves.

In The Well-Rested Woman, Janet Kinosian examines how pregnancy creates a fundamental conflict between habitual sleep posture and the medically recommended SOS (Sleep On Side) position. Kinosian (2002) identifies proper pillow support as the most evidence-supported mechanical solution to this conflict — noting that it works not by forcing a position, but by making the correct position feel natural and stable throughout the night, so the body stops fighting to return to old habits.

Key Takeaway

The goal of a pregnancy pillow isn't cushioning — it's postural anchoring. By filling the gaps between your body and the mattress in SOS position, a good pillow eliminates the physical discomfort that triggers unconscious rolling. Left-side sleeping improves placental blood flow and reduces swelling; a pillow that supports the belly, back, and knees simultaneously makes that position sustainable all night.

Why the SOS Position Matters

The inferior vena cava runs along the right side of the lumbar spine. When a pregnant woman lies flat on her back, the weight of the growing uterus — which can reach 2–3 kg by the third trimester — directly compresses this vessel. The effect is measurable: studies using Doppler ultrasound have shown reduced fetal heart rate variability in supine positioning after 28 weeks.

Left-side sleeping (SOS left) is preferred over right-side for two reasons: it keeps the uterus off the vena cava, and it maximises blood and nutrient delivery through the renal arteries. That said, right-side sleeping is not dangerous and is far preferable to supine positioning — don't panic if you wake up on your right. The practical goal is simply to avoid sleeping on your back.

From 20 weeks onward, every OB-GYN will give the same advice. The challenge is execution across eight or nine hours of sleep.

What Changes Trimester by Trimester

First Trimester (Weeks 1–12)

The uterus is still tucked behind the pubic bone, so positional pressure on major vessels is minimal. Most first-trimester discomfort is back pain from hormonal ligament relaxation (relaxin is released from week 6), nausea disrupting sleep onset, and breast tenderness making certain positions painful. A small wedge or a standard body pillow for knee support is usually sufficient.

Second Trimester (Weeks 13–26)

The belly becomes visible between weeks 14 and 16 for most women, and belly support becomes relevant by week 20. Round ligament pain — sharp pulling sensations along the lower abdomen — can make position changes feel risky. Back pain worsens as the lumbar curve deepens to accommodate the growing uterus. This is the trimester where most women make the move to a dedicated pregnancy pillow.

Third Trimester (Weeks 27–40)

Full-body support becomes essential. The belly is large enough to create significant side-to-side torque when unsupported, pulling the lower back even during attempted side sleeping. Heartburn is common — slight head elevation (not just pillow stacking, but a controlled wedge under the upper body) reduces reflux. Hip pressure on the mattress increases. Leg cramps and restless leg syndrome peak. A quality C- or U-shape pillow addresses all of these simultaneously.

Temperature Considerations

Pregnancy increases basal body temperature by approximately 0.2–0.5°C from early in the first trimester due to progesterone. By the third trimester, the metabolic load of carrying a near-term fetus can make sleeping feel uncomfortably warm even in a cool room. A full-body pregnancy pillow adds significant insulating mass to one side of the body.

Look for pillows with covers in breathable cotton jersey or bamboo-derived rayon. Avoid polyester microfibre covers — they trap heat and feel clammy. The best covers are removable and machine washable: pregnancy means more sweating, and a pillow cover that can't be washed frequently will become unhygienic quickly.

After Birth: Your Pillow Doesn't Retire

A C- or U-shape pregnancy pillow repurposes directly into a nursing support pillow. Positioned around the waist and lap, it holds the infant at breast height without requiring the mother to lean forward — a posture that causes the shoulder and upper back pain that nearly every nursing mother experiences in the first weeks. The Boppy brand built its entire identity on this repurposing concept.

Even outside of nursing, a full-length body pillow remains useful postpartum: many women find that the hip and pelvic girdle pain from the loosened ligaments of late pregnancy persists for several weeks after delivery. The same between-knees positioning that helped during pregnancy continues to reduce hip rotation strain while the pelvis stabilises.

Bottom Line

Start with a C-shape like the Leachco Snoogle for second-trimester belly support. If you reach the third trimester needing back support as well, upgrade to a U-shape. If you share a full or queen bed and space is the constraint, two Boppy wedges solve 80% of the problem in half the footprint. Whatever you choose, prioritise a removable, washable cover and a fill density that won't compress flat within a few weeks.
