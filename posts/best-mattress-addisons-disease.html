The Addison's Disease Sleep Science

  • Primary adrenal insufficiency (Addison's disease): autoimmune destruction of the adrenal cortex eliminates production of both cortisol and aldosterone -- the two hormones most critical to sleep-wake regulation and orthostatic blood pressure control
  • Cortisol deficiency breaks the diurnal HPA rhythm: in healthy individuals, cortisol surges between 6-8 AM to signal waking; in Addison's disease this dawn surge is absent, causing profound morning fatigue, difficulty rising, and unrefreshing sleep regardless of hours slept
  • Aldosterone deficiency causes sodium and fluid loss overnight, reducing blood volume by morning -- producing orthostatic hypotension (blood pressure drop on standing) that creates dizziness, near-fainting, and fall risk during morning rising
  • Adrenal crisis risk is highest in the early morning, when cortisol reserves from replacement doses are depleted -- physical exertion from difficult rising or falls can trigger a life-threatening cortisol crisis in undertreated patients
  • Temperature dysregulation: cortisol and aldosterone both modulate vasomotor tone and sweating efficiency -- their absence causes cold intolerance, night sweats, and poor thermal adaptation that fragment sleep architecture throughout the night
  • Muscle weakness and fatigue from cortisol deficiency reduce the patient's ability to reposition during sleep and to push up from a deep-sinking mattress surface during rising -- surface firmness and edge support directly affect energy expenditure and safety
  • Secondary adrenal insufficiency (pituitary ACTH failure) shares cortisol deficiency and HPA disruption but retains aldosterone function -- orthostatic hypotension and sodium wasting are minimal, making it a distinct clinical profile from primary Addison's disease

Addison's Disease vs. General Fatigue vs. Chronic Fatigue Syndrome: Why the Mattress Priorities Differ

General fatigue is a symptom requiring comfort and pressure relief -- any medium-soft mattress addresses it. Chronic Fatigue Syndrome (ME/CFS) involves post-exertional malaise and autonomic dysfunction -- mattress criteria focus on minimal repositioning effort and temperature management. Addison's disease has a specific endocrine mechanism: (1) cortisol-deficiency fatigue that is worst in the morning and correlates directly with the gap between replacement dose timing and the natural dawn cortisol surge, (2) aldosterone-deficiency orthostatic hypotension requiring edge support and gradual rising capability -- a patient who cannot easily sit up on the mattress edge risks a fall or blood pressure crisis, and (3) adrenal crisis vulnerability from physical exertion, making a mattress that requires excessive muscular effort to rise from a genuine medical safety concern. Firmness, edge support, and adjustable base compatibility are clinical requirements -- not comfort preferences -- for this population.

Frequently Asked Questions

How does Addison's disease affect sleep quality?

Addison's disease disrupts sleep through three primary mechanisms. First, cortisol deficiency breaks the normal diurnal cortisol rhythm -- in healthy individuals, cortisol begins rising around 3-4 AM to prepare the body for waking, but in Addison's disease this dawn surge is absent or blunted, causing profound morning fatigue and difficulty rising. Second, aldosterone deficiency causes sodium and fluid loss overnight, dropping blood volume and contributing to orthostatic hypotension -- the blood pressure drop on standing that can cause dizziness or near-fainting on morning rising. Third, the HPA axis dysregulation that defines primary adrenal insufficiency disrupts the stress-response signaling that normally coordinates sleep stage transitions, increasing nighttime arousals and reducing restorative slow-wave sleep. Patients frequently report unrefreshing sleep regardless of duration.

What mattress features help with orthostatic hypotension from Addison's disease?

Orthostatic hypotension in Addison's disease -- caused by aldosterone deficiency reducing blood volume and vascular tone -- creates a specific morning rising risk. The mattress should support a gradual position change protocol: sitting up with legs over the side before standing, allowing blood pressure to stabilize before full weight-bearing. This requires a mattress with enough edge support that the patient can sit upright on the edge without sinking or rolling, and firm enough surface response that repositioning from lying to sitting does not require excessive muscular effort. A mattress that is too soft causes the patient to sink deeply, making it much harder to push up to seated from within a foam envelope. Adjustable bases that raise the head gradually while still lying flat are particularly useful -- they allow partial tilt recovery before the patient attempts to stand.

Does temperature regulation matter for Addison's disease sleep?

Temperature dysregulation is a direct consequence of adrenal insufficiency. Cortisol and aldosterone both play roles in thermoregulation -- cortisol affects vasomotor tone and heat dissipation, while aldosterone regulates the sodium and fluid balance that underpins sweating efficiency. Patients with Addison's disease frequently experience both cold intolerance and paradoxical heat dysregulation during sleep, which manifests as night sweats on warmer nights or chilling on cooler nights. Both fragment sleep architecture. A mattress with active heat dissipation -- open coil hybrids, phase-change material covers, or polymer grid construction -- reduces the thermal load on the sleep surface, minimizing temperature-driven arousals. Dense all-foam mattresses without cooling technology amplify the dysregulation already present from adrenal insufficiency.

What is the difference between primary and secondary adrenal insufficiency for mattress selection?

Primary adrenal insufficiency (Addison's disease) involves destruction of the adrenal cortex itself -- typically from autoimmune attack -- causing deficiency of both cortisol AND aldosterone. Secondary adrenal insufficiency involves failure of the pituitary gland to produce ACTH, causing cortisol deficiency but NOT aldosterone deficiency (aldosterone is regulated primarily by the renin-angiotensin system, not ACTH). For mattress selection, orthostatic hypotension and sodium wasting are features of PRIMARY insufficiency due to aldosterone loss -- edge support, morning rising support, and adjustable base zero-gravity positioning are therefore most critical for Addison's disease specifically. Both forms share cortisol-deficiency fatigue, HPA axis sleep disruption, and temperature dysregulation, so pressure relief and cooling criteria apply to both.

Should someone with Addison's disease use an adjustable base?

An adjustable base is strongly beneficial for Addison's disease for two reasons. First, for orthostatic hypotension management: the zero-gravity position (head and feet elevated, body at a neutral angle) reduces gravitational blood pooling in the legs during sleep, helping maintain central blood volume overnight -- particularly relevant for aldosterone-deficient patients who are already volume-depleted. Rising from a partially elevated position is also mechanically easier than rising from flat, reducing the abruptness of the postural change that triggers blood pressure drops. Second, for fatigue management: patients with cortisol-deficiency fatigue have difficulty transitioning from lying to standing. An adjustable base that props the patient to a reclining seated position reduces the muscular effort required to rise, protecting energy expenditure in patients for whom every physical demand draws on a critically limited reserve. The mattress must be confirmed adjustable-base compatible by the manufacturer before purchasing a base.
