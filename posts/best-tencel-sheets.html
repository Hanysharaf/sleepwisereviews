The Science of Tencel: Why It Sleeps So Cool

Tencel (Lyocell) is produced by dissolving wood pulp — usually eucalyptus — in a non-toxic solvent called NMMO (N-Methylmorpholine N-oxide). The solution is extruded through spinnerets to create individual fibers, and 99% of the solvent is captured and reused in a closed-loop process. This gives Tencel a dramatically lower environmental footprint than conventional cotton (which uses 2,700 liters of water per T-shirt equivalent).


The fiber's round, smooth cross-section creates two cooling advantages: (1) capillary action efficiently wicks moisture from skin to fiber surface, where it evaporates; and (2) the smooth surface produces less friction heat than the irregular surface of cotton or linen. A 2021 study in the Journal of Textile Science found Tencel absorbs moisture 50% faster than cotton and releases it 30% faster, measurably reducing skin surface temperature during the first 90 minutes of sleep — when thermoregulation is most critical for deep sleep onset.

How to Choose Tencel Sheets

100% Tencel vs. Blends

Pure Tencel (100% Lyocell) delivers maximum moisture-wicking, softness, and sustainability. Blends with polyester cost less but reduce breathability and lifespan. Blends with cotton are more durable but stiffer. For hot sleepers: 100% Tencel is the only real choice.

Weave: Sateen vs. Percale

Tencel sateen is silkier, drapier, and slightly warmer. Tencel percale is matte, crisper, and cooler. Both are more breathable than cotton equivalents. Hot sleepers should default to percale weave; those who prioritize feel over temperature should choose sateen.

Certifications That Matter

OEKO-TEX Standard 100: confirms no harmful chemicals in the finished product. GOTS (Global Organic Textile Standard): the gold standard for organic fiber sourcing and processing. CleanBamboo: ettitude's proprietary closed-loop bamboo certification. These are the only certifications worth paying a premium for.

Pocket Depth

Most standard mattresses need a 14-inch pocket. Hybrid mattresses with toppers may need 16-18 inches. Check your mattress depth before ordering — a fitted sheet that slips off at 3am defeats the purpose of premium bedding.

Care: What Ruins Tencel Fast

Hot water (shrinks and weakens fibers), high-heat drying (same issue), fabric softener (coats fibers and reduces wicking), and bleach (destroys fiber structure). Wash cold, gentle cycle, tumble dry low or air dry. Tencel requires minimal care effort beyond temperature control.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is Tencel fabric?

Tencel (brand name for Lyocell) is a fiber made from wood pulp — typically eucalyptus — in a closed-loop process where 99% of the solvent is recycled. The result is a silky, breathable, moisture-wicking fiber that is gentler on skin than cotton and more sustainable than most natural fibers.

Are Tencel sheets cooler than cotton?

Yes. Tencel is hygroscopic — it actively draws moisture away from your skin and disperses it across the fabric surface for evaporation. Studies show Tencel can absorb 50% more moisture than cotton, making it significantly cooler for hot sleepers and especially effective during the first 90 minutes of sleep.

Do Tencel sheets pill?

Quality Tencel sheets pill far less than cotton because the smooth, rounded fiber profile has fewer fiber ends that can fray and ball up. Lower-grade Tencel blends may pill if combined with short-staple cotton or polyester. 100% Tencel from a reputable brand should remain smooth for 3-5 years with proper care.

How do you wash Tencel sheets?

Wash on a cold, gentle cycle with mild detergent. Avoid hot water and high-heat drying — Tencel can shrink or wrinkle under heat. Tumble dry low or air dry. Skip fabric softener; Tencel stays naturally soft without it, and softener coats the fibers and reduces moisture-wicking performance over time.

Is Tencel safe for sensitive skin?

Yes. Tencel is naturally smooth with no sharp fiber ends, and its moisture-wicking properties prevent the damp microenvironment that irritates sensitive skin. Dermatologists often recommend OEKO-TEX certified Tencel for eczema and sensitive skin. Avoid blends with polyester if skin sensitivity is a primary concern.
