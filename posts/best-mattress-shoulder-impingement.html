Shoulder Impingement Sleep Science

  • Subacromial space anatomy: Normal acromiohumeral interval 7–14 mm; impingement symptomatic when <6 mm; lateral decubitus (side lying) on affected shoulder reduces interval to near zero
  • Night pain mechanism: Prolonged side sleeping on the affected shoulder → sustained subacromial compression → supraspinatus ischemia + bursal inflammation → nocturnal pain that peaks 2–4 hours into sleep
  • Internal rotation worsening: Arm-across-chest or arm-under-body positions internally rotate the humerus → further narrows subacromial outlet → explains why many patients report pain specifically in these positions
  • Arm-overhead effect: Reaching overhead during sleep (as occurs in REM) rotates the greater tuberosity under the acromion → mechanical impingement even without lying on the shoulder
  • Back sleep decompression: Supine position widens acromiohumeral interval → scapula protracts on a flat surface → posterior tilt of acromion → subacromial space opens 2–4 mm vs. lateral decubitus
  • Mattress firmness and sinkage: Side sleepers need sufficient shoulder sinkage to allow the humeral head to drop below acromion level; firm surfaces prevent this and maintain compression
  • Rotator cuff tendon health: Supraspinatus is avascular at the critical zone (1 cm from insertion) — repetitive nocturnal compression further impairs perfusion and delays tendon healing
  • Corticosteroid injection window: Post-injection (48–72 hrs) is highest-risk for tendon weakening; surface support during this window is clinically important
  • Post-SAD recovery: After subacromial decompression (arthroscopic acromioplasty), back sleeping for 4–6 weeks is standard; a mattress that facilitates neutral arm positioning accelerates tendon healing
  • Acromion morphology: Type II (curved) and Type III (hooked) acromia have smaller native outlet than Type I (flat); these patients have less tolerance for surface-induced compression during sleep

Frequently Asked Questions

What is shoulder impingement and why does sleep make it worse?
Shoulder impingement syndrome occurs when the supraspinatus tendon and subacromial bursa are compressed between the humeral head and the acromion. During side sleeping on the affected shoulder, the subacromial space compresses to near zero, inflaming the bursa and tendon with every micro-movement. Arm-overhead or across-chest sleep positions further narrow the outlet by internally rotating the humerus into the acromion — causing the characteristic night pain that peaks 2–4 hours into sleep and is often severe enough to wake the patient.
Should I sleep on the side of my shoulder impingement?
No. Side sleeping on the affected shoulder is the worst position — it compresses the subacromial space, loads the inflamed bursa, and maintains the shoulder in internal rotation that narrows the outlet. Back sleeping is optimal: it allows the shoulder blade to rotate posteriorly, widens the subacromial space by 2–4 mm, and distributes weight away from the rotator cuff. If back sleeping is not possible, side sleeping on the unaffected shoulder with a body pillow supporting the impinged arm in neutral (not across the chest) is the next best option.
What mattress firmness is best for shoulder impingement?
Medium to medium-soft (4.5 to 5.5 out of 10) for side sleepers with impingement: enough shoulder sinkage to allow the humeral head to drop below acromion level, decompressing the subacromial space. Too firm a surface keeps the shoulder elevated against the acromion. For back sleepers with impingement, medium-firm (6 to 6.5) is appropriate as back sleeping provides inherent subacromial decompression regardless of shoulder sinkage depth.
Can a mattress help after shoulder impingement surgery?
Yes. Post-arthroscopic subacromial decompression (SAD) or acromioplasty patients require a surface that maintains the arm in neutral rotation during sleep, prevents inadvertent arm-overhead drift during REM sleep, and supports comfortable back sleeping through the early recovery period (4–6 weeks). A slow-recovery foam or an adjustable base mattress that resists position change and supports arm pillow placement is ideal for the post-operative trajectory.
What sleep position prevents arm-overhead drift that worsens impingement?
Back sleeping with arms at the sides or resting on the abdomen prevents arm-overhead drift and keeps the shoulder in external rotation and slight abduction — the open-packed position that maximizes subacromial space. A slow-recovery foam mattress (TEMPUR material) creates a positional nest that resists REM sleep drift toward lateral positions and overhead arm extension. An adjustable base set to 15–20° head elevation also helps maintain back sleeping throughout the night by making the position mechanically stable.
