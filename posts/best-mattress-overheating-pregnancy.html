Best Mattress for Overheating During Pregnancy (2026)

Updated May 2026 • 7 picks • Trimester-by-trimester guide

Pregnancy raises your core temperature, blood volume, and metabolic rate simultaneously. The mattress you sleep on becomes the single biggest environmental factor in nighttime temperature. This guide covers the 7 coolest mattresses tested for pregnancy heat, plus a trimester-by-trimester breakdown of what your body is actually doing and why it matters for mattress choice.

Why Pregnancy Causes Overheating at Night

A 2019 study in Sleep Medicine Reviews documented that basal metabolic rate increases 20% during pregnancy due to fetal growth demands. Blood volume rises 45% by the third trimester, forcing the cardiovascular system to work harder and generating excess heat. Progesterone elevates the hypothalamic temperature set point, shifting the body's definition of "normal" temperature upward. Result: you are physiologically warmer by design, and standard mattresses amplify the problem.

Trimester-by-Trimester Heat Profile

First Trimester (Weeks 1-12)

  • Progesterone spike starts
  • Mild overheating begins
  • Any sleep position still safe
  • Invest now: mattress takes time to break in

Second Trimester (Weeks 13-26)

  • Blood volume peaks at +45%
  • Heat generation increases sharply
  • Side sleeping recommended from week 20
  • Best window to buy a new mattress

Third Trimester (Weeks 27-40)

  • Maximum heat production
  • Left side only (improves fetal circulation)
  • Hip and shoulder pressure critical
  • Cooling + zoned support both essential

Sleep Position Safety Note

After 20 weeks, the American College of Obstetricians and Gynecologists recommends sleeping on the left side. This position takes pressure off the inferior vena cava (the major vein returning blood to the heart), improves fetal circulation, and reduces kidney pressure. Any mattress you choose should provide adequate shoulder and hip cushioning for sustained left-side sleeping. A mattress that is too firm creates pressure points that force position changes throughout the night — disrupting sleep further.

Cooling Technology Comparison

🔥

Traditional Memory Foam

Traps body heat in dense cell structure. Avoid if overheating is your primary concern.

♨️

Gel Memory Foam

Cooler than standard memory foam. Gel beads dissipate heat but saturate over time.

💨

Latex & Open-Cell Foam

Natural airflow through cell structure. Latex especially effective — stays cooler than foam alternatives.

❄️

Hybrid / Innerspring

Coil layer creates continuous airflow channels. Best cooling of all mattress types. Priority pick for pregnancy.

Frequently Asked Questions

Why do pregnant women overheat at night?

Pregnancy raises basal metabolic rate by 20% and blood volume by 45%, both generating excess heat. Progesterone also elevates the hypothalamic temperature set point. The combination is most intense in the second and third trimesters.

What mattress type is coolest for pregnancy?

Innerspring and hybrid mattresses sleep coolest because coil layers allow free air circulation. Natural latex runs cooler than synthetic foam. Avoid traditional all-foam mattresses if heat is the primary concern.

Is firm or soft better during pregnancy?

Medium firmness (5-6 out of 10) is generally best. Too firm creates hip and shoulder pressure in the required side-sleeping position. Too soft lacks the spinal support needed as weight distribution changes. Zoned mattresses adapt automatically.

When is the best time to buy a mattress during pregnancy?

The second trimester (weeks 13-26) is ideal. Past early nausea, blood volume has peaked, side sleeping is becoming necessary, and there are several months to adapt before delivery. Third-trimester purchases leave little adjustment time.

Do cooling toppers help with pregnancy overheating?

Toppers can improve surface temperature by 2-4 degrees. They work best over innerspring or hybrid bases. If your current mattress is all-foam, a new hybrid mattress delivers significantly more cooling than any topper placed on top of dense foam.
